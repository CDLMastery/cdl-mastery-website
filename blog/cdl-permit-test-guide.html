CDL Permit Test Beginner Guide

Understand the permit process, choose the right knowledge tests, and build a study plan that prepares you for the next step.

10-minute read Updated July 17, 2026

Getting a commercial driver’s license can feel complicated when you first see the classes, endorsements, restrictions, knowledge tests, training rules, and skills-test requirements. The process becomes much easier when you separate it into stages: decide what you want to drive, study your state manual, pass the required knowledge tests, obtain your commercial learner’s permit, complete applicable training, practice safely, and pass the CDL skills test.

Start by choosing the CDL class you need

Your CDL class depends on the type and weight of vehicle you plan to operate. Before studying, confirm whether your intended job requires a Class A, Class B, or another licensing path under your state’s rules.

  • Class A generally covers combination vehicles that meet the applicable weight thresholds.
  • Class B generally covers qualifying heavy single vehicles and certain vehicles towing lighter units.
  • Class C may apply to certain passenger or hazardous materials vehicles that do not meet Class A or Class B definitions.
Before you study Check job listings or speak with the employer or training provider you plan to use. Studying for the wrong class or missing a required endorsement can delay your application.

Use your current state CDL manual

CDL testing follows federal standards, but state licensing agencies administer the tests. Each state publishes its own CDL manual, and that manual should be your primary study source.

Pay attention to your manual’s sections on application requirements, documentation, fees, testing procedures, endorsements, restrictions, and state-specific rules. Do not rely only on an old manual, a random answer sheet, or another state’s requirements.

Understand which knowledge tests you need

Most first-time applicants begin with the general knowledge test. Additional tests depend on the vehicle, equipment, cargo, and work you plan to perform.

Possible test areas include:

  • General Knowledge
  • Air Brakes
  • Combination Vehicles
  • Hazardous Materials
  • Tank Vehicles
  • Doubles and Triples
  • Passenger Transport
  • School Bus

For example, someone pursuing a Class A license for a tractor-trailer equipped with air brakes will commonly need General Knowledge, Combination Vehicles, and Air Brakes. The exact tests depend on the license, endorsements, restrictions, and state requirements involved.

Do not memorize answers without understanding them

Practice questions are useful, but they work best when they reinforce the manual instead of replacing it. Learn why the correct answer is correct and why the other options are unsafe or incomplete.

Organize your study into small topics such as inspections, shifting, speed management, following distance, railroad crossings, emergency procedures, cargo safety, and braking systems.

Common mistake Repeating the same practice test until the answers look familiar can create false confidence. Mix questions, review explanations, and return to the manual whenever you miss a concept.

Use a simple study cycle

A strong beginner routine can follow five steps:

  1. Read one short section of your state CDL manual.
  2. Write down the major safety rules and key relationships.
  3. Answer practice questions only for that topic.
  4. Review every incorrect answer and explanation.
  5. Retest the topic later without looking at your notes.

Continue until you can explain the rule in your own words. That is a stronger sign of readiness than recognizing a familiar answer choice.

Prepare your documents before visiting the licensing agency

Your state may require proof of identity, residency, citizenship or lawful presence, Social Security information, medical qualification, fees, and other documents. Requirements can vary, so check your state licensing agency before your appointment.

Applicants who operate in categories subject to federal medical certification requirements may need a valid Medical Examiner’s Certificate from a certified medical examiner.

What happens after you pass the knowledge tests?

After meeting your state’s requirements and passing the required knowledge tests, you may receive a commercial learner’s permit, often called a CLP.

The CLP allows supervised practice under the conditions established by federal and state rules. It is not the same as a full CDL and does not authorize unsupervised commercial driving.

Under the current federal process, an applicant generally must hold the CLP for at least 14 days before becoming eligible for the CDL skills test. Your state may have additional requirements.

Know when ELDT applies

Entry-Level Driver Training requirements apply to many applicants seeking a Class A or Class B CDL for the first time, upgrading from Class B to Class A, or obtaining certain endorsements for the first time.

Required training must be completed through a provider listed on the FMCSA Training Provider Registry. The provider reports completed training before the applicant can take the applicable test.

ELDT applicability depends on the license or endorsement being sought and the applicant’s previous licensing history. Confirm your status through official FMCSA and state resources.

Prepare for all three parts of the skills test

Passing the permit knowledge tests is only one stage. The CDL skills test generally includes:

  • Vehicle Inspection Test: demonstrate that you can inspect safety-critical vehicle components.
  • Basic Controls Test: demonstrate controlled vehicle movement and backing maneuvers.
  • Road Test: demonstrate safe operation in traffic and real roadway conditions.

Practice in the class and type of vehicle appropriate for the license and restrictions you are pursuing.

Avoid these beginner mistakes

  • Studying from an outdated CDL manual.
  • Taking practice tests without reading explanations.
  • Ignoring endorsements and restriction rules.
  • Waiting until the last minute to gather documents.
  • Assuming the permit test is the final CDL test.
  • Using another state’s process as your only guide.
  • Scheduling training without checking ELDT eligibility.

Your beginner preparation checklist

  • Choose the correct CDL class.
  • Identify every endorsement you need.
  • Download the current manual from your state.
  • Confirm application and medical requirements.
  • Study one section at a time.
  • Use practice questions to find weak areas.
  • Pass all required knowledge tests.
  • Obtain and safely practice with your CLP.
  • Complete applicable ELDT requirements.
  • Prepare for inspection, controls, and road testing.
Best first move Download your state’s current CDL manual and write down the exact class, endorsements, and tests required for the driving job you want.
